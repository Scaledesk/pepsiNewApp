Treasury Scope

As Sales is the face of the company for Customers, R&D for innovation, Reporting for Sector, Treasury is the face of the company for Banks. Treasury is the part of all activities where Banks act as key Stakeholders. Treasury in a company is key in determining the firm’s financial strategy and financial policy – advising on what businesses to invest in, organizing the appropriate funding for this, and controlling the risk in the organization.

India Treasury Team - Organization Structure

Treasury Department in PepsiCo India Region is a part of Finance Control Team stationed at the PepsiCo India headquarters – Gurgaon. The team looks after all treasury related <p/> operations in India Region - India, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka and Nepal.

The team is  lead by India Treasurer. Treasury Manager reports to India Treasurer while Treasury Consultant (Operations) and Treasury Consultant (Exports and Insurance) report to Treasury manager.

Cash Flow Operations India Region

Treasury facilitates:

  • Payments

Treasury forms the final leg of all kinds of Payments.

Treasury does the following

  1. Decides which bank to be co-ordinated for Payments depending on Criteria like – Relationship, Charges, IT Infrastructure, Processing Time, etc.
  2. Ensures local/foreign payments are as per RBI norms
  3. Ensures payment approvals are as per Authority Matrix & Board Resolution.
  4. Ensure smooth processing of advances & urgent payments
  5. Ensures funds are available for smooth transactions.
  6. Ensures smooth cross-country payments (buying/selling functional currencies & paying off)

Treasury in association with other finance verticals, have outsourced the payments. Today, IBM’s  P2P team takes care of invoice processing (normal/advances/urgent), discrepancy reporting, reconciliation, and even posting of entries in our books of accounts.

Treasury should be approached in one of the following cases.

  1. Queries in regards to rightful approver for any payment
  2. New/Exceptional/unique payments which requires a feasibility check as per RBI guidelines
  3. Queries in regards to pre-requisites or documents required for successful completion of transactions
  4. Queries on urgent payments, for example, on-time payment to government bodies

Another Critical area of Cash flow Operations is Collections

Treasury team co-ordinates from the flow of money from Distributors or Modern Trade Partners & On-premise outlets to Bank.

Treasury does the following:

  1. Maps collections accounts to various distributors as on when created.
  2. Maps collections to respective GLs for clarity in recording in the books of accounts.
  3. Intimates about Bank holidays and other days when collections can be disrupted.

Treasury should be approached in one of the following cases

  1. When units require clarity – If PepsiCo has received funds against a particular claim (Kindly note, Treasury has provided access to units and IBM with the viewing rights in Banks Portal)
  2. Programs like Channel Financing and NACH as a product </p> their details and implementation.

Cash Management

In Cash Management, Treasury is responsible for the cash line in the Balance Sheet.

The team does in the following:

  1. Forecast on Payment and collection, </p> both Short term and long term
  2. Ensuring availability of funds for thousands of payments on a daily basis.
  3. Monitoring Debt Position by timely and correct fund transfers. This is to ensure maximum interest saving.
  4. Monitoring Cash Position by investing surplus cash
  5. Buying/Selling of non-functional currency at a reasonable rate
  6. Circulating Bank Statements to concerned stakeholders for internal reconciliation with the books of accounts.

Forex & Hedging

  • Forex

The imports payments are in non-local currencies like USD, AUD or EURO.

Treasury  plays a pivotal role:

  1. To Buy or Sell non-local currency at reasonable rates.
  2. To reduce currency risk and minimize Forex Loss
  3. To Ensure all documents are in place for internal audit.
  4. To Correctly represent of all such transaction in books of account.

For Example

Import payments & Export collections are in USD, AUD, EURO, JPY , BDT . Apart from import and exports, there are inter country cross-charges in foreign currency. In such scenario, PepsiCo is exposed to currency risk or transactional risk.

  • Hedging

Hedging is analogous to insuring. Hedging ensures guaranteed inflow/outflow of cash at a particular rate, provides a clearer picture about our investments and/or payments. In this VUCA world, PepsiCo takes calculative decision on where to hedge. Hedging in India is largely governed by the corporate FX team.

Example – Here is an example where Hedging addresses the exposure of a futuristic payment.

Assume, PepsiCo INC have provided a loan of USD 20MM to PepsiCo India to facilitate expansion of business in India. PepsiCo India promises to repay the same on some later date (say 3 years from now). India business converts this foreign currency (USD) to local currency (INR) and  uses the same to expand the business.

One can clearly observe that 3 years is enough to have huge uncertainty on the exchange rate. One learning is INR has devalued from INR 45/USD in 2011-12 to INR 65/USD in 2014-15. The dollar repayment to PepsiCo INC is hence hedged as per the repayment schedule. Hedging cost is generally around 8%. The team also works closely with bank to ensure all future cross-currency transactions are rightly hedged.

Note – Treasury assesses the exposure (currency risk in the above example) before going ahead with the hedging.

MCLR Rates & Interest Rates

  • Interest Rate

The interest is charged on the tenure of the loan taken. Generally, interests rates are higher for Overnight (debt with 1 day maturity) rates as they are daily loans. The interest on OD and Working capital Loan is charged cumulatively at the end of the month.

  • MCLR Rates

The Marginal cost funds based Lending Rate is a new regime envisioned by the RBI of India, applicable from 1st April, 2016. Under this regime, the rate of interest will be charged differently for different tenures. The standard tenures are:

  1. Overdraft
  2. 1 Month
  3. 3 Month
  4. 6 Month, and
  5. 12 Months

Additionally, this rates will change on a month basis. To add to that, the date of declaration of MCLR rates varies from bank to bank. The treasury keeps a track of all the rates by closely coordinating with the Banks. Banks charge PepsiCo, a premium on the MCLR rates. This rates are decided by banks, and often subjected to negotiation by Treasury. Treasury India generally negotiates the interest charges just over the MCLR rates.

Import & Exports

In this module, we will capture various aspects of :

  • Imports
  • Exports

Import

PepsiCo makes  tons of  payments against imports every year. The import are not confined to neighboring countries alone, but  on a global scale. There are various currency in which the payments are done and is hence governed by stringent legislation and monitoring by Banks and RBI. All imports are majorly classified into two separate buckets:

  1. A1 Payments – Goods for example Raw Material or Packing Material or Capex related like buying  Machine parts
  2. A2 payments – Services e.g. Installation of machineries

Each payment moves through a series of processes, right from raising PO to ensuring the goods are delivered and the services are rendered //Change Animation of Service. There are innumerous documentation that goes with each payment. Couple of such documents are

  1. 15 CA
  2. 15 CB
  3. PO
  4. Invoices
  5. Statutory Documents
  6. Bill of Entry
  7. Air Waybill or Bill of Lading

Out of the previously mentioned documents, 15CA/15CB are the certification from the authorized auditors confirming the legitimacy of the transaction. This is one of the most important and mandatory document necessary for any import payment.

The other documents like PO, Invoices, Bill of Entry, Airway Bill (if imported by air cargo), Bill of Lading (if imported by a ship), etc. are also necessary and should clearly depict the nature , type, amount, currency, volume, etc. of the transaction. Each document is verified from the point of dispatch till it reaches the necessary unit. There are also statutory commitments like customs duty on each import made.

Treasury does the following in any import payment:

  1. Keeps track of all import requests.
  2. Confirms each documents are in place.
  3. Keeps track of all documentation & submits the same to Banks/RBI.
  4. Does Customs duty payments.
  5. Collates and submits the bill of entry to Banks.
  6. Co-ordinates with IBM & Units to ensure all payments are correct and done in time.
  7. Ensures funds are available for each kind of payment.
  8. Ensures minimal forex loss or conversion loss during payments.
  9. Co-ordinates with Units to with documentation and confirmation/receipts/challans for smooth payments.
  10. Co-ordinates with bank if any exceptional import payment is flagged off.
  11. In association with Bank conducts audit for import payments

The exports to these countries are governed by different agreements and bilateral legislation between both the countries. Treasury ensures that all payments are rightly followed and transactions are smooth. Similar to imports, </p> in exports there are multiple documentations involved. Examples of these can be as follows:

  1. PO
  2. Invoice
  3. Letter of Credit
  4. Certificate of Origin
  5. Shipment Bill
  6. Truck Receipt
  7. Radioactivity certificate

All documents mentioned earlier and handled or provided by  multiple third party vendors/auditors/statutory body. These documents should be collated and confirmed post which a request for transfer of goods can be initiated. A good 10-15 documents from 5-6 sources is received per transaction. All documents are signed at treasury end and submitted to relevant stakeholders.

Treasury also keeps track of all such LC and dispatches. Treasury works closely with the  team to ensure smooth transactions and processing.  Moreover, one of the criticalities of Export is the currency handled. The collections in export business is in dollars  for Bangladesh and Sri Lanka, while in Indian rupees for Nepal. To add on that, the point of realization of sale is different for different countries .

Due to multiple documents involved and co-ordination with various banks and various stakeholders, both import and export needs to be handled carefully. Treasury acts as a facilitator to ensure smooth cross country transactions while maintaining 100% compliance to regulatory requirement.

Supplier Financing

Supplier financing is a solution incorporated in the payment side of the business. This works on the arbitrage of cost of Debt between the Supplier & PepsiCo. The Vendor faces additional cost while PepsiCo benefits in DPO improvement. The final payment terms are generally decided during the agreement with the suppliers. The process is then Simple.

1) Buyer(PepsiCo) issues a PO to Supplier

2) Supplier delivers goods to Buyer(PepsiCo)

3) Supplier raises an invoice and sends to IBM for processing. IBM processes the invoice and uploads in Bank’s portal

4) Bank discounts the supplier’s receivables and credits their existing accounts immediately

5) Bank debits PepsiCo’s account with invoice value on the due date

Note:

Cost of Funding for Vendors ~ 11% - 13%

Cost of Funding for PepsiCo ~ 8%

The arbitrage of 3%-5% is transferred to PepsiCo as increase in Payment Terms thereby increasing DPO

You may reach out to Treasury Team for more details  of the program

NACH

Before understanding NACH, let us understand the current Collection process

Current Process

  1. Distributer places  an order for supply of goods with PepsiCo.
  2. PepsiCo accepts the sales Order
  3. Distributor pays PepsiCo in one of following modes
    1. Cheque or Demand Draft
    2. NEFT or RTGS
  4. Once funds are received, PepsiCo releases the order and supplies the goods to the distributor.

Treasury Projects

In 2016, Treasury helped implement NACH (National Automated Clearing House) process to smoothen this collection process.

In NACH, the distributer provides PepsiCo the authority to debit Distributer’s account . The credit is automatically transferred in PepsiCo’s books once the collection is received

NACH is one of the most convenient methods of fund transfer in the world of automation. It is a hand-off solution for distributers and provides ease of fund transfers. Treasury in association with  commercial and BIS, drives the project.

NACH Program Highlights

  • Automated solution for billing, inventory management and collection
  • Reduction in time spent by sales force on following up for payments
  • Reduction in delays of orders by removal of human involvement in payment
  • Reduction in error in RTGS/NEFT/cheque/Demand draft

Channel Financing

PepsiCo today deals with innumerous distributers. To support these distributers in their working capital, PepsiCo extends support by tying up with Banks and helping these distributers to get debt at lower cost.

While distributers get Bank loans at ~ 12% to 15%, PepsiCo helps Distributers by issuing a letter of comfort to get these Bank loans at ~ 8% to 10%.

This project is hence mutually beneficial

On one hand Distributers get Bank loans at lower rate while in the other PepsiCo has relatively better DSO as distributers pay in time
